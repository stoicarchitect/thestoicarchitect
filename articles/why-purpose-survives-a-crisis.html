Why Purpose Is the Only Thing That Survives a Crisis

When everything external is stripped away, what remains is either an anchor or a void. The men who rebuild fastest are not the ones with the most resources.

Daniel Brown MSc
7 min read
March 2026
Faith · Pillar I

In 2019, I was made redundant. I was newly married, a new father, and the financial architecture I had built my life around collapsed in the same month. What followed was not a clean, manageable crisis. It was a cascade — the kind where one thing falling takes three others with it.

I have spoken to enough men since then to know that this pattern is not unusual. The specific triggers are different — redundancy, separation, illness, failure — but the experience is the same. Everything you believed was stable turns out to have been load-bearing. And when it goes, you discover, sometimes for the first time, what was actually underneath it.

For most men, what's underneath is either an anchor or a void.

The men who rebuild fastest are not the ones with the most resources. They are the ones with the clearest sense of why they are here.

·

Purpose Is Not What You Think It Is

When men hear the word "purpose," most of them immediately think of career. Of legacy. Of the thing they want to be remembered for. These are not wrong — but they are downstream of something deeper.

Purpose, at its most structural, is the answer to a single question: Who are you when you have nothing left to prove?

Career can be stripped. Income can be stripped. Status, reputation, relationships — all of it can go. What cannot be stripped is the internal architecture of a man who knows, at a bone-deep level, what he is for. Not what he does. What he is.

"A man with a strong enough why will always find the how. A man without one will be undone by the first serious obstacle he encounters."

This is not motivational language. It is structural observation. I have watched capable men — genuinely talented, driven men — collapse in crisis not because they lacked the skills to recover, but because they had built their entire identity on external scaffolding. When the scaffolding came down, there was nothing holding them up from the inside.

The Identity Trap

Most men build their identity from the outside in. They become their job title. Their income bracket. Their relationship status. Their gym numbers. Their followers.

This is not vanity — it is the natural result of a culture that measures men by what they produce. From the moment we are old enough to be asked "what do you want to be when you grow up," we are trained to locate our worth in external outputs.

The problem is not that these things matter. The problem is that they are all temporary. Every external measure of a man's value is subject to forces he cannot fully control. The economy shifts. The company restructures. The relationship ends. The body ages. The market changes.

A man whose identity is entirely external has no stable ground to stand on when those external things shift. He mistakes the scaffolding for the building. When the scaffolding comes down, he believes the building has collapsed — even when the foundations are still intact beneath him.

The F5IVE Framework — Faith (Pillar I)

In the F5IVE Framework, Faith is the foundation beneath everything else. It is not the first pillar because it is the most important in isolation — it is first because without it, none of the other pillars have a stable base. Purpose anchors discipline. Identity anchors decision-making. Values anchor behaviour under pressure.

What an Anchor Actually Looks Like

I want to be precise here, because the concept of "purpose" is frequently used in a way that makes it sound like something you find on a vision board weekend. It is not.

An anchor is built from three things:

  • A clear sense of your values — not the ones you claim to hold, but the ones demonstrated by your actual decisions under pressure. These are not the same thing for most men.
  • An identity that is internally located — not "I am a director" or "I am a provider," but "I am a man who operates with integrity and builds things that last." The second version survives redundancy. The first does not.
  • A relationship with something larger than yourself — this may be faith in the religious sense, or it may be a deep sense of responsibility to family, community, or a long-term mission. The form varies. The function is the same: it provides a reference point that exists outside of immediate circumstances.

None of these are quick to build. None of them can be acquired in a moment of crisis. That is why this pillar comes first. The anchor has to be built before the storm, not during it.

The Rebuild

When my own scaffolding came down in 2019, what I found underneath was not the void I feared. It was rougher than I expected, less polished — but it was there. A set of values I had not fully articulated but had been operating from for years. A sense of responsibility to my son that was not contingent on my job title or my bank balance. A faith that, even in its most tested form, gave me a reference point that the crisis could not reach.

I do not tell this to make the rebuild sound easy. It was not. The five years that followed were the hardest of my life. But the men I watched struggle most during their own crises were not the ones with the fewest resources. They were the ones who had never asked themselves — in any serious way — who they were when everything else was taken away.

The question is not academic. It is the most practical question a man can ask himself. Because the crisis is coming. Not if — when. And the only preparation that matters is the architecture you have built before it arrives.

The Question

If everything external were stripped away tomorrow — the title, the income, the reputation, the relationship — who would you be?

If the answer comes easily and clearly, you have an anchor.

If the question produces anxiety, or a long silence, or a list of things you own rather than things you are — that is where the work starts.

Not on your goals. Not on your habits. On the foundation beneath all of it.

That is what the Faith pillar is for.
